Word Definition

The Word Definition Area is the part of the display that shows the definition of a word; the word is either that entered in the Input Field, or that clicked in the Word List Area.

The definition shown has several features based upon the markup of the underlying digitized dictionary.

  • The headword is shown in larger type at the beginning of the definition.
  • The definition is shown in several records; in general, these correspond to different senses of the definition.
  • Accompanying each record is a location reference for the record within the scanned image of the text. For example [p=39,2] indicates that the record is found on page 39 in column 2 of the text. By clicking on the page number, the user may view a scanned image of the page. This facility is especially useful when the user suspects some error in the digitization. When such errors are found, the user is encouraged to click the CORRECTIONS button and submit a correction to us.
  • Also shown is an internal record identifier (for example [L=7391]). A user submitting a correction should reference this 'L' number, so we will know exactly what needs correcting.
  • Monier-Williams organized the words of the dictionary into four 'lines'; see the description on Page xiv of the Preface in the scanned edition. These lines are represented by the parenthetical element beginning most records. For instance, (H1) indicates that the record is in the first of Monier-Williams' four lines of words. Records without these 'line' indications correspond to alternate senses of a previous record whose 'line' indication is shown.
    Some line indicators have a suffix B or C shown. The 'B' usually indicates that the record is for a different gender of the nominal form. A 'C' usually indicates that the record is an indeclineable 'inflected' form of the preceding word. For instance, if one looks at the definition for 'agra', she will see '(H1C) agram ind. in front'.
  • Most records also have an elaborated rendition of the headword. This elaboration may include accents for vowels, and hyphens. The hyphens provide clues regarding the construction of the word from simpler forms. For example, the first record in the definition for the word anuśaya shows the form anu-śaya, indicating that the word is a compound of the words anu and śaya. In this case, the interested user can find more information by examining the definitions for these two words. While the Monier-Williams dictionary does not provide etymologies with the specificity of the dictionary by Wilson, such explorations of the compound components from MW are one way the user can enhance her experience of the MW dictionary.
  • The body of each record typically has many abbreviations. These appear as text in a different color; when the mouse cursor is positioned over such text, a brief explanation of the abbreviation is shown.
  • Another type of abbreviation occurs for references to Sanskrit texts and authors. These are shown as links rendered as underlined text. When the user clicks on such a link, a popup window of 'MWAuthorities' appears, scrolled to the particular reference.
  • Records sometimes have references to related words in other languages. For those words in Greek, a link is provided for the Greek text. This link is to the Perseus Greek Word Study Tool, and may be useful to those students familiar with both Greek and Sanskrit. For an example, see the word 'agre', which appears as a record in the definitions for 'agra'.
  • For the Sanskrit roots, links to two sources of further information are shown where available.
    Whitney Roots provides a link to an indexed scanned edition of William Dwight Whitney's The Roots, Verb-Forms, and Primary Derivatives of the Sanskrit Language. See the root 'gam' for an example.
    Westergaard Dhatupatha provides links to a similar edition of of N. L. Westergaard's Radices Linguae Sanscritae; this is the book to which Monier-Williams Dhatupatha references correspond. See the root 'yam' for an example.
